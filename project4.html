Red-Winged Blackbird Rocket: Detailed Overview

Context

This rocket is a hybrid between the L1 I launched and the L2 I plan to launch in April. I wanted to gain more experience with desinging, building, and launching rockets before going for my L2 certification.

Content

I am designing Red-Winged Blackbird to familiarize myself with working on avionics and composites. It will be flying on the I435T at LUNAR. I am using a pre-assembled Eggtimer Quasar AV board with dual-deploy parachute and GPS tracking capability and have 3D printed my AV sled and endcaps. I integrated it into my rocket, which I had never worked with before. I learned how to make my own nosecone with fiberglass sleeves and epoxy, including how to safely cut and sand the fiberglass. Additionally, I CADed and printed the shoulder of my nose cone which now slides into my upper airframe. This rocket is using a pin switch, which I chose because it requires a little more design consideration than a magnet switch or a screw switch.

Conclusion

Red-winged Blackbird was successfully launched and recovered, flying to an apogee of 4282 ft, hitting Mach 1.59, and pulling an average of 37 Gs. One small challenge I had was with the fiberglass nosecone I built. I thought the fiberglass layers would be thicker and overcompensated when I CADed my jig. I ended up with a lip between the nosecone and the upper airframe. Since the airframe was just cardboard on this rocket, I was worried the force of thrust on take-off would cause the nosecone to deform the airframe. I didn't have the time or materials to remake the nosecone, so I CADed a shoulder that transitioned between the nosecone and the airframe and 3D printed it with strong carbon fiber-reinforced filament. Overall, I had a lot of fun designing, building, and launching this rocket! I learned custom nosecone making, sanding and cutting epoxy, and general avionics.
